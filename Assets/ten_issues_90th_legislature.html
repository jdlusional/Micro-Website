Ten Issues to Watch in the 90th Legislature

A nonpartisan look at the policy questions most likely to dominate the floor when Texas lawmakers reconvene, drawn from what passed, what did not advance, and what has stayed contested since the 89th session.

Convenes: January 12, 2027 Adjourns: May 31, 2027 Pre-filing opens: November 2026 Ranked by: likely floor time & intensity of opposition
How to read this. Texas meets in regular session every two years, so no 90th-session bills exist yet. The measures listed under each issue are the 89th-session vehicles, including companions, that were enacted, vetoed, or left unfinished. 01THC and hemp regulation
Why it makes the cut

▸A full ban passed the Senate three times across the regular and two special sessions, but the House never reconciled a final version, and Gov. Abbott vetoed the ban and issued a regulatory executive order in its place.▸Supporters say prohibition is necessary to protect children from unregulated, high-potency products that have flooded the market through a loophole in the 2019 hemp law.▸Critics say an outright ban would destroy a multibillion-dollar legal industry, eliminate tens of thousands of jobs, and push consumers toward unregulated alternatives.

02Property tax relief
Why it makes the cut

▸The 89th session delivered homestead exemption increases ratified by voters in November 2025, while structural reform bills limiting local taxing authority did not advance in the House. The lieutenant governor has branded his 2027 proposal "Operation Double Nickel," which would lower the homestead freeze age to 55 and raise exemptions to $180,000.▸Supporters say expanding exemptions provides direct, measurable relief to homeowners facing some of the highest property tax burdens in the nation.▸Critics say exemption-based relief erodes as appraisals rise, shifts the tax burden onto renters and businesses, and may not be fiscally sustainable long-term.

04Redistricting and quorum rules
Why it makes the cut

▸A mid-decade congressional redistricting effort triggered a House Democratic quorum break that collapsed the first special session before new maps passed in the second. The House subsequently adopted new rules penalizing future quorum breaks through fines, seniority loss, and budget cuts.▸Supporters say the Legislature has clear constitutional authority to redistrict at any time and the new penalties ensure elected members fulfill their obligation to be present for votes.▸Critics say the maps were drawn to predetermine electoral outcomes rather than reflect communities of interest, and the penalties suppress a legitimate procedural tool of the minority.

▸Voters approved SJR 5 in November 2025, allowing judges to deny bail for certain violent felonies, but two additional constitutional amendments targeting undocumented immigrants and repeat offenders fell short of the House supermajority threshold. About this briefing

Issues are ranked by a combined judgment of likely floor time and the intensity of opposition each generated through the 89th regular and two called sessions and the 2026 interim. Higher-ranked issues combine leadership attention, unresolved status, and the most organized cross-pressure; the THC, property tax, and education topics lead because each pairs top-tier leadership attention with active, unsettled conflict, including the rare split between Abbott and Patrick on THC.

Because Texas convenes biennially, no 90th-session bills exist yet; filing opens in November 2026. The measures cited are 89th-session vehicles, including companions, that mark where each debate now stands and the most likely starting points for 2027. Vote counts and statuses reflect the regular and called sessions as reported by the Texas Legislature Online, chamber press offices, and contemporaneous reporting.

Nonpartisan Analysis

This was prepared as a nonpartisan and neutral analysis. The framing here aims to present what passed, what did not advance, and what remains contested without endorsing any position.

Bill statuses and interim charges are current as of this publication 2026 and may change as committees report and pre-filing begins.
